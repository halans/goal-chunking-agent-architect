# The Harada Method — reference

A concise, accurate primer on the method this skill is built on. Read this if you need to
explain *why* the worksheets are shaped the way they are.

## Origin

The Harada Method was developed by **Takashi Harada**, a teacher and track-and-field coach
in Osaka, Japan. In the early 1990s he took over the track program at a junior-high school
in one of Osaka's most deprived districts — the worst-performing school out of ~380 in the
city. Rather than drilling technique harder, Harada focused on the athletes' **inner
world**: purpose, self-reliance, and disciplined daily habits. Over the following years his
students went on to win **13 national gold medals**, and the school became #1. He later
systematized the approach and has since trained tens of thousands of people and hundreds of
companies (including large Japanese firms).

The method reached a global audience through **Shohei Ohtani**, who at age 15 completed his
own version of the central chart — an 8×8 grid whose goal was to be drafted #1 by 8 pro
teams, with focus areas including physical conditioning, control, mental strength, and even
"luck" (with sub-actions like picking up trash and greeting people). It is a canonical
example of the method in action.

> Details above are drawn from public Harada Method materials (theharadamethod.com,
> haradato.com, frakxion.com, harada-educate.jp) and widely reported accounts of Ohtani's
> chart. Treat specific figures as approximate.

## Core philosophy

- **Self-reliance.** The individual takes full responsibility for their goal and their
  growth. Success is *built*, not granted by talent or luck.
- **Consistency over intensity.** Meaningful goals are reached through small, repeatable
  daily actions, not sporadic bursts of effort.
- **Performance and character together.** The method develops *who you are* (attitude,
  gratitude, service to others) alongside *what you achieve*. "Sharpening the heart" is
  treated as inseparable from sharpening skill.
- **Purpose before plan.** You clarify *why* the goal matters and *who you must become*
  before decomposing *what* to do.

## The five instruments

Used together, these five forms create a complete, self-reinforcing system.

### 1. Long-Term Goal Setting Sheet
A deep, structured goal-clarification exercise. It typically captures:
- The goal itself, stated concretely and measurably, with a deadline.
- **Purpose / meaning** — why this goal matters, to you and to others.
- The **four-quadrant analysis** of the goal's value, crossing two axes:
  *tangible ↔ intangible* and *for yourself ↔ for others*. (A goal that serves all four
  quadrants is far more motivating and durable.)
- Analysis of **past** results (successes and failures and their causes) and an honest read
  of **current** ability.
- **Supporters** — who helps you, and how you will give back to them.
- The **values / attitude** you will hold.

### 2. Open Window 64 (OW64) — the mandala / "64 Chart"
A **9×9 grid** (81 cells) built from a 3×3 of 3×3 blocks:
- The **center cell** holds the **main goal**.
- The **8 cells** immediately around the center hold **8 core themes** (pillars) — the
  areas you must master to reach the goal.
- Each of those 8 themes is then copied into the center of one of the 8 outer blocks and
  surrounded by **8 concrete actions**.
- Result: **8 themes × 8 actions = 64 actionable items** that fully decompose the goal.

This is the heart of the method: it forces a big, vague goal into 64 specific, doable
things across balanced dimensions.

### 3. Routine Check Sheet
A daily tracking sheet. You select the highest-leverage **routines** (often pulled from the
64 actions) and mark each day whether you did them. It converts intentions into habits and
makes consistency visible and self-enforcing.

### 4. Daily Diary
A short daily reflection: what happened, what you learned, gratitude, and the focus for
tomorrow. It builds self-awareness and continuous course-correction.

### 5. Self-analysis / self-reliance foundation
The connective philosophy and practice: taking responsibility, anticipating obstacles and
pre-deciding countermeasures, and cultivating the attitude (honesty, gratitude, service)
that sustains the whole system.

## Why it transfers to agents

An agent reaching an objective faces the same problem a person does: a goal that is too
big and vague to act on directly, a need to work reliably rather than heroically, and a
need to recover from setbacks without giving up. The Harada instruments are, in effect, a
proven decomposition-and-discipline pipeline. `references/mapping.md` shows exactly how
each instrument becomes a part of an agent.
